What is HIV?

The Human Immunodeficiency Virus

HIV (Human Immunodeficiency Virus) is a retrovirus that attacks the body's immune system — specifically the CD4 cells (T cells) — weakening the ability to fight infection and disease. Without treatment, HIV can progress to AIDS (Acquired Immunodeficiency Syndrome), the most severe stage.

3 Stages of HIV Infection
Stage 1 — Acute HIV Infection
Develops 2–4 weeks after infection. Flu-like symptoms appear. Viral load is very high and transmission risk is greatest. Many people don't realize they have HIV at this stage.
Stage 2 — Chronic HIV Infection (Clinical Latency)
HIV multiplies at low levels. May last a decade or longer with treatment. People may have no symptoms but can still transmit the virus. ART can keep people in this stage for decades.
Stage 3 — AIDS (Acquired Immunodeficiency Syndrome)
CD4 count drops below 200 cells/mL. Immune system is severely damaged. Without treatment, survival is about 3 years. Opportunistic infections become life-threatening.
Key Vocabulary

Important Terms to Know

HIV
Human Immunodeficiency Virus — the virus that causes AIDS.
AIDS
Acquired Immunodeficiency Syndrome — the final, most severe stage of HIV infection.
CD4 Cells
T-lymphocytes targeted by HIV. Low CD4 count (under 200) signals AIDS.
Viral Load
The amount of HIV in the blood. Lower = better. Undetectable = untransmittable.
ART
Antiretroviral Therapy — daily medications that suppress HIV replication.
PrEP
Pre-Exposure Prophylaxis — medicine taken by HIV-negative people to prevent infection.
PEP
Post-Exposure Prophylaxis — emergency medication taken after possible HIV exposure.
Undetectable
Viral load so low it can't be measured. Person cannot sexually transmit HIV.
Opportunistic Infection
Infections that occur because HIV has weakened the immune system.
Retrovirus
A type of virus that inserts its RNA into host cell DNA to replicate.
Seroconversion
The point when HIV antibodies become detectable in the blood after infection.
U=U
Undetectable = Untransmittable — a scientifically confirmed prevention concept.
How HIV Spreads

Transmission Routes

HIV is spread through specific body fluids from a person who has HIV. These fluids must enter the bloodstream, a mucous membrane, or damaged tissue of an HIV-negative person.

⬆
Sexual ContactMost common route. Anal or vaginal sex without protection. Receptive anal sex carries the highest risk.
⬆
Sharing Needles / SyringesInjecting drug use with shared equipment allows direct blood-to-blood transmission.
⬆
Mother to Child (Perinatal)Can pass during pregnancy, childbirth, or breastfeeding. Treatment dramatically reduces this risk.
⬆
Blood TransfusionRare in the U.S. due to thorough screening of the blood supply since 1985.
HIV Cannot Spread Through
✕
Casual ContactHugging, shaking hands, sharing dishes or toilet seats does not transmit HIV.
✕
Saliva, Tears, or SweatThese body fluids do not contain enough HIV to transmit the virus.
✕
Insects or MosquitoesExtensive CDC research has found no evidence of HIV transmission through insects.
✕
Air or WaterHIV does not survive long outside the human body and cannot be transmitted this way.

Stage 2 — Clinical Latency

Often No Symptoms

During chronic HIV infection, many people have no symptoms at all. This stage can last a decade or more with proper treatment. However, the virus is still active and can be transmitted to others.

Stage 3 — AIDS

Severe Immune Damage

At this stage, the immune system is severely compromised. Symptoms can include rapid weight loss, recurring fever, extreme fatigue, prolonged diarrhea, pneumonia, memory loss, depression, and mouth/skin sores. Opportunistic infections and certain cancers (like Kaposi's sarcoma) become common.

Getting Tested

HIV Testing & Diagnosis

The only way to know your HIV status is to get tested. The CDC recommends everyone aged 13–64 get tested at least once, and those at higher risk get tested more frequently.

Antibody Tests (Most Common)

Detect antibodies the body produces in response to HIV. Results available in 20–30 minutes with rapid tests. Most accurate 23–90 days after exposure (the "window period").

Antigen/Antibody Tests

Recommended for labs and some rapid tests. Can detect both HIV antibodies and the p24 antigen. Can identify HIV sooner — within 18–45 days of exposure.

Nucleic Acid Tests (NAT)

Look for actual HIV in the blood. Can detect HIV 10–33 days after exposure. Used for people with recent high-risk exposure or early symptoms, or to confirm other positive results.

Free, confidential HIV tests are available at health departments, community health centers, and many pharmacies. At-home test kits are also available.

Managing HIV

Treatment Options

There is no cure for HIV, but antiretroviral therapy (ART) allows people with HIV to live long, healthy lives and prevents transmission.

Antiretroviral Therapy (ART)

A combination of HIV medicines taken daily. ART suppresses HIV so it cannot replicate, lowers viral load to undetectable levels, protects the immune system, and prevents progression to AIDS. The goal is viral suppression to under 200 copies/mL — ideally undetectable.

PrEP (Pre-Exposure Prophylaxis)

A daily pill for HIV-negative people at higher risk. When taken consistently, PrEP reduces the risk of getting HIV from sex by about 99%, and from injection drug use by over 74%.

PEP (Post-Exposure Prophylaxis)

Emergency medication taken within 72 hours of possible HIV exposure. Must be taken for 28 days. The sooner it's started, the more effective it is.

Other Prevention Tools

Consistent and correct condom use, never sharing needles, regular STI testing, and choosing lower-risk sexual behaviors all reduce HIV transmission risk.

If Left Untreated

Possible Complications

Without ART, HIV progressively weakens the immune system, leading to AIDS and life-threatening conditions:

Opportunistic InfectionsPneumocystis pneumonia (PCP), tuberculosis, toxoplasmosis, and cytomegalovirus that a healthy immune system would normally fight off.
Certain CancersKaposi's sarcoma, lymphoma, and cervical cancer occur at higher rates in people with AIDS due to a weakened immune system.
Neurological ComplicationsHIV can cause dementia, memory loss, depression, and anxiety. About 50% of people with HIV experience neurological issues.
Wasting SyndromeInvoluntary weight loss of 10%+ of body weight combined with diarrhea, fever, or weakness for more than 30 days.
Cardiovascular DiseasePeople with HIV have a higher risk of heart disease and metabolic complications, even with treatment.
Type 2 DiabetesPeople with HIV/AIDS are up to 4 times more likely to develop type 2 diabetes than those without the virus.
With TreatmentART dramatically reduces all of these risks. People on effective ART can achieve a near-normal life expectancy.
